The Problem

Good opportunities were getting lost before they even started

In financial services, a well-known friction point exists at the very beginning of the process. Before any formal review starts — before underwriting, before legal, before compliance — there's often a murky stage where no one is quite sure if an opportunity is ready, who should look at it first, or what information is even needed.

A small business owner doesn't know what documents to prepare. A relationship manager isn't sure which team to involve first. An executive doesn't have a quick way to gauge if something is worth their time yet. The result is extra back-and-forth that slows everyone down — and it happens before the real work has even begun.

🔄
Too many unnecessary handoffs
Opportunities were bouncing between departments before anyone had a clear picture of what was actually being requested.
📋
Applicants arrived unprepared
Small business owners and developers didn't know what to expect — leading to incomplete submissions and delays.
🕐
Slow early-stage decisions
Without a shared framework, early alignment between relationship managers, compliance, and executives took too long.
🤷
No clear "next step" for borderline cases
Opportunities that weren't clearly yes or no had no structured path forward — they just stalled.
What I Built

A front door — not a replacement for the people inside

Clearpath is a three-screen guided assessment that takes any opportunity — a business loan request, a commercial real estate deal, an internal bank initiative, or a community program — and produces a full strategic readiness report in under 5 minutes.

It's intentionally simple to use, but the output layer is designed for executives. Think of it like a triage tool in a doctor's office: a nurse asks you a few questions before you see the doctor. The nurse isn't diagnosing you — they're making sure the doctor has the right information before walking in the room. The difference here is that the "report" the doctor receives reads like a strategic brief, not a form.

How the Scores Work

No AI. No black box. Just honest math.

This is important: Clearpath does not use artificial intelligence. There's no machine learning, no algorithm trained on data, and no automated lending decision. Every score is calculated using a simple, transparent set of rules — the same way a checklist works, just made visual.

Here's the simplest way to explain it:

1
You answer 6–8 questions about your opportunity — things like how long the business has been open, how consistent the revenue is, or what the collateral situation looks like.
2
Each answer gets a point value. A business open for 10+ years scores higher on readiness than one open for 6 months. Strong revenue scores higher than inconsistent revenue. That's it.
3
Those point values are averaged and combined to produce a score out of 100 for each dimension. Think of it like a report card — each category gets its own grade.
4
The Decision Style toggle (Conservative / Balanced / Growth-Oriented) adjusts the weight of those scores slightly — like adjusting how strictly a teacher grades. Conservative = stricter, Growth-Oriented = more generous on upside potential.
What each score actually measures
Readiness Score
How prepared is this opportunity right now? Based on factors like time in business, collateral, and how confident the applicant is in their ability to repay. Think: is this ready to walk in the door?
Risk Score
How much could go wrong? A higher number means lower risk. It's the inverse — a 90 means this looks low-risk, not high-risk. Driven by repayment confidence and revenue stability. Think: how worried should we be?
Growth Potential
Is this opportunity on an upward trajectory? Based on revenue trends and market demand. Think: is this going somewhere?
Relationship Value
How strong is the existing relationship with the institution? A long-standing customer scores higher than someone brand new. Think: do we know this person?
Deposit Opportunity
Could this lead to deposits or other banking business? Based on relationship depth and revenue size. Think: is there more business here?
Community Impact
Does this opportunity serve the broader community? Particularly relevant for Hawaii's community reinvestment goals. Think: does this help our neighbors?
Scope & Boundaries

What Clearpath does not do — on purpose

The most important design decision in this product was deciding what to leave out. Clearpath is a supplement to human judgment, not a substitute for it.

⚠️ Clearpath does not replace — and is not designed to replace:

  • 🏦 Underwriting. Clearpath doesn't approve or deny loans. It helps identify whether an opportunity is ready for underwriting to review.
  • ⚖️ Legal and compliance review. Any legal or regulatory assessment still requires qualified professionals.
  • 📊 CRM or core banking systems. This tool doesn't connect to loan origination systems, credit bureaus, or financial data systems — that would require a full enterprise integration.
  • 👤 Human decision-makers. The "Send for Manual Review" button exists for a reason. People make the final call — always.
  • 🤖 AI or machine learning. No AI is used anywhere in this system. Every score is produced by transparent, auditable, rule-based logic.
What's Next

What a real enterprise integration would require

This prototype demonstrates the product concept and UX. A production version for a financial institution would require the following additional investment:

Now →
Front-end prototype (this): Demonstrates the full user experience, scoring logic, department routing, and export functionality. Suitable for stakeholder alignment and concept validation.
Phase 2
Secure API connections: Tie scoring inputs to verified data sources — bank relationship history, internal CRM data, and deposit records — to reduce manual input and improve accuracy.
Phase 2
Role-based access controls: Bank employees, relationship managers, and executives would each see a different view — with appropriate data visibility based on their role.
Phase 2
Audit logging: Every assessment, score change, and routing decision would be logged for compliance and review purposes.
Phase 3
Core banking system integration: Connect to loan origination systems (e.g. nCino, Salesforce Financial Services Cloud) to pass structured data directly into formal review workflows.
Phase 3
Compliant AI layer (optional): Once the rules-based foundation is validated, a private, auditable AI layer could assist with document review — with full human oversight and explainability requirements.

The Bigger Picture

Clearpath is one layer. Senior executives need both.

Clearpath handles the front door — routing, readiness, early alignment. But the people at the top of a financial institution are thinking at a completely different level. A Chief Credit Officer isn't asking "is this one loan ready?" They're asking: "What does our entire portfolio look like if we keep approving loans like this one?"

That's a different tool. It's the difference between evaluating a single opportunity and managing the health of hundreds of them at once.

Layer 1 — Operational
Clearpath
For relationship managers, borrowers, and business owners. Answers: "Is this opportunity ready to move forward?"
Audience: Front-line staff, applicants, internal teams
Layer 2 — Executive
Credit Portfolio Strategy
For credit officers and executives. Answers: "What happens to our whole portfolio if conditions change?"
Audience: CCOs, credit teams, executive leadership

Together, these two tools cover the full lifecycle — from the moment an opportunity walks in the door to how it affects the institution's overall risk and return picture. That's the system a well-run financial institution actually needs.

Strategic Takeaway

Why this matters beyond the tools themselves

Most people build one tool and call it a product. The more interesting question is: how do tools at different levels of an organization connect to tell a complete story?

Clearpath and the Credit Portfolio tool aren't just two demos — they're a demonstration of how to think across the full decision lifecycle. From the borrower walking in, to the relationship manager routing the deal, to the CCO evaluating what that deal means for the institution's portfolio health.

That kind of systems-level thinking — across UX, operations, and financial strategy — is what separates a product that gets built from a product that actually gets used.

"The strongest systems don't replace good judgment. They make sure good judgment gets the right information at the right time."
— Design principle behind Clearpath
